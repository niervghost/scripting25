Dublin

Geography

Dublin is situated on a lowland plain the mouth of the River Liffey on Ireland's east coast. The city is bounded to the south by the stunning Wicklow Mountains, which also form a half-moon shape along the Irish Sea. With flat or moderately sloping terrain to the north and west and greater height to the south, the city is separated into a Northside and a Southside by the River Liffey. The Liffey River runs east through the city to Dublin Bay, where it joins the Tolka and Dodder rivers.

The Northside and the Southside are the two halves of Dublin, which are separated in the city centre by the River Liffey. In general, the Southside is more upscale, while the Northside is more working class. While there are a few exceptions, generally speaking, this is a useful guideline to follow when visiting the city. This split may elicit strong feelings from Dubliners on both sides, and it is the subject of many jokes and astute remarks you may hear in conversation.

Dublin, a small European metropolis, has a unique village atmosphere with a mix of regional styles and ambiances. Its center is a ten-minute bus trip from O'Connell Street or College Green, offering a variety of towns and villages with unique personalities. The city offers a blend of old Victorian magnificence in coastal town Bray and upscale rugby-playing Ballsbridge. To read some of Dublin's most attractive locations, head south along Dublin Bay, which features sandy beaches, rugged cliffs, coves, harbors, and seaside communities. Sandycove, Dalkey, and Killiney have preserved their old-world charm, while Howth, a significant fishing harbor, and Malahide, a charming coastal town with a park and 19th-century castle, are located north of Dublin.
